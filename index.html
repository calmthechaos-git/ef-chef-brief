Part 4 — Adversarial Views

What will push back —
and how to neutralize it.

Every objection has a neutralizer. Read these before writing a word of copy.

From Your Audience
Objection
"A summit won't fix my kid's eating"
Why It Exists
Parents in crisis want solutions, not information. Watching talks feels passive.
Neutralizer
Don't promise to fix anything. Promise "a starting point — what to look for in professionals, and ideas to support yourself or your kids."
Objection
"Free summits are pitch-fests"
Why It Exists
Every free summit sells a $997 course in the last 10 minutes.
Neutralizer
Set explicit ground rules: no pitching. State this upfront: "No sales pitches. Just real conversations."
Objection
"The format itself overwhelms me"
Why It Exists
Video fatigue, audio processing demands — the summit could trigger the challenges it addresses.
Neutralizer
Captions on everything, low-stimulation visuals, no flashing graphics. Make it a feature: "The first ND-designed summit."
From the ND Community
Objection
"Nothing about us without us"
Why It Exists
If all speakers are neurotypical clinicians talking ABOUT ND people, there will be backlash.
Neutralizer
Ensure ND speakers are on the lineup as experts — not just "patient stories." You, as a late-diagnosed autistic host, already embody this. Make it explicit.
Objection
"ARFID isn't something you 'fix'"
Why It Exists
Some advocates reject the idea that eating challenges need to be "solved." They want accommodation, not intervention.
Neutralizer
Don't promise "recovery" or "normal eating." Promise understanding, safety, and practical support. Your framing already handles this.
From Potential Speakers
Objection
"Who is Rachel Le?"
Why It Exists
You have 3 Substack posts and a small Facebook following. Speakers will Google you and see thin credibility.
Neutralizer
Lead with the gap, not yourself: "There are zero parent-facing summits in this space. I'm building the first one." The landing page gives you professional web presence.
